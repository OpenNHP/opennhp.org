The Internet Is Becoming a Dark Forest:
Why AI Changes Network Security

NHP
The OpenNHP Team
February 21, 2026 Security AI

In Liu Cixin's science fiction trilogy The Three-Body Problem, the universe operates by a single, terrifying rule: any civilization that reveals its location will be destroyed. The safest strategy is silence. Invisibility. The universe is a dark forest—and so, increasingly, is the Internet.

This week, two product launches crystallised something security professionals have been watching build for years. On February 19, an open-source project called PentAGI quietly went public on GitHub. On February 20, Anthropic announced Claude Code Security—and cybersecurity stocks fell off a cliff. These two events, taken together with a growing body of academic research, signal that we have crossed a threshold. AI has made attack capabilities cheap, fast, and accessible to anyone. The equation is now simple: if you are visible, you are vulnerable.

The Tools Are Here

PentAGI is not a proof-of-concept paper. It is a fully autonomous, open-source penetration testing system that anyone can clone from GitHub and run today. Built by VXControl, it coordinates a team of specialised AI agents—one for reconnaissance, one for coding, one for infrastructure—that work together to independently discover, analyse, and exploit vulnerabilities. It ships with over 20 integrated security tools (nmap, metasploit, sqlmap, and more), runs in an isolated Docker environment for safety, and can use any major LLM—OpenAI, Claude, Gemini, or a locally-hosted Ollama model. The barrier to running a sophisticated penetration test has collapsed to a git clone.

Claude Code Security is different in kind but identical in implication. Anthropic's new Opus 4.6 model, embedded directly into the Claude Code development environment, reads and reasons about codebases the way a senior security researcher would—tracing data flows, understanding component interactions, surfacing complex logic vulnerabilities that pattern-matching tools miss entirely. In internal testing on open-source software running across enterprise systems and critical infrastructure, Opus 4.6 found vulnerabilities that had gone undetected for decades. When the announcement dropped on February 20, markets reacted immediately: CrowdStrike fell 8%, Cloudflare dropped 8.1%, Zscaler shed 5.5%, and Okta declined 9.2%. The market was pricing in a world where AI finds vulnerabilities faster than companies can sell you tools to detect the resulting breaches.

The Research That Got Us Here

Neither of these products appeared from nowhere. The research trajectory has been clear for two years.

Apr 2024 UIUC: LLM agents exploit 87% of one-day CVEs autonomously.

University of Illinois researchers showed that GPT-4 could autonomously exploit 87% of real-world one-day vulnerabilities when given only a CVE description—compared to 0% for open-source scanners like Metasploit. Paper →

Nov 2024 Google Big Sleep: first real-world vulnerability found by an AI agent.

Google's Project Zero and DeepMind collaboration discovered a stack buffer underflow in SQLite—a real, exploitable vulnerability—before it appeared in any official release. Google Project Zero →

Apr 2025 Imperva: automated traffic exceeds human traffic for the first time.

Bad bots now account for 37% of all internet traffic—up from 32% the prior year. For the first time in a decade, automated traffic (51%) surpassed human traffic (49%). AI is lowering the floor for bot sophistication while raising the ceiling. Imperva Report →

May 2025 OpenAI o3 discovers a Linux kernel zero-day with no scaffolding.

Security researcher Sean Heelan used o3 directly via API—no agentic framework, no tooling—to discover CVE-2025-37899, a remote zero-day use-after-free in the Linux kernel's SMB implementation. Sean Heelan's blog →

Aug 2025 AI generates working CVE exploits in 10–15 minutes, at $1 per exploit.

The traditional patch window—measured in weeks—has collapsed to minutes. Exploits for newly published CVEs are being generated automatically and made publicly available. Cyber Security News →

Dec 2025 Stanford ARTEMIS outperforms 9 of 10 professional penetration testers.

Stanford's ARTEMIS AI agent achieved 82% accuracy in a live enterprise environment—at $18/hour versus $60/hour for human pentesters. The researchers concluded that AI hackers are "dangerously close" to exceeding human capabilities. Stanford paper →

And now, in February 2026: an open-source autonomous attack agent freely available to anyone, and an AI from Anthropic that finds vulnerabilities humans have missed for decades. The research phase is over. This is production.

Visibility = Vulnerability

The CrowdStrike Global Threat Report tells the same story from the defender's perspective. Mean time to full compromise fell from 7 minutes in 2022, to 2 minutes 7 seconds in 2023, to 51 seconds in 2024—an 88% reduction in three years. Over the same period, new vulnerabilities tracked by NVD grew from 25,000 to 40,000 per year. The math is unforgiving: faster exploits, more attack surface, less time to respond.

88% reduction in three years. The detection-and-response window has effectively closed.

The conventional response—detect, alert, patch—was already struggling. It assumed human-speed attacks against a manageable number of vulnerabilities. Neither assumption holds today. When a working exploit can be generated in 15 minutes for $1, and when autonomous agents are already running 24/7 scanning the Internet, the detection window has effectively closed.

"In the age of GenAI, preemptive capabilities—not detection and response—are the future of cybersecurity." — Gartner, September 2025

There is a precise word for what Gartner is describing: invisibility. If the service cannot be found, it cannot be scanned. If it cannot be scanned, no CVE exploit—however cheap and fast to generate—can be applied to it. The attack surface is not hardened; it is removed.

The Solution: Invisibility by Default

This is the founding premise of OpenNHP—the Network-resource Hiding Protocol. Rather than trying to detect and block attacks faster than AI can launch them, NHP makes your services invisible to anyone who has not first been cryptographically verified.

The model is simple but radical. By default, every port, every service, every IP address is dark. Nothing responds. Nothing is listed in any scan. An attacker—whether human, bot, or autonomous AI agent—sees nothing to attack. Only after an NHP-Agent presents a valid, cryptographic identity does the NHP-Server grant a time-limited token and instruct the Access Controller to open a connection. The window is narrow, targeted, and logged. The rest of the Internet never sees it.

This is not a new idea wrapped in new marketing. NHP is grounded in a formal architecture that has been developed alongside and validated by two of the world's most rigorous standards bodies:

  • Cloud Security Alliance (CSA) — NHP is the technical foundation of the CSA's Stealth Mode SDP for Zero Trust Network Infrastructure specification, which defines how Zero Trust Network Access should be implemented at the protocol level.
  • IETF — The NHP protocol is being formally standardised as draft-opennhp-saag-nhp on the IETF standards track, the same body that defined TCP/IP, HTTP, TLS, and DNS.

OpenNHP is the open-source implementation of this protocol. It is available now, under the Apache 2.0 licence, for any organisation to deploy.

The Forest Is Getting Darker

PentAGI and Claude Code Security are not the end of this story. They are the point where attack capabilities became freely available to everyone, including actors with no security background and no budget. The democratisation of offensive AI was always going to happen. It has happened.

The question every organisation must now answer is the same one Liu Cixin posed at a cosmic scale: in a forest where any signal draws a hunter, what is the rational strategy?

The answer is the same whether you are a civilisation in the universe or a server on the Internet. Don't be visible.
